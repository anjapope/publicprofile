## Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy focuses on developing critical capacities related to the social and technical systems that shape contemporary life and society. Across history, information science, digital humanities, and librarianship, I emphasize learning pathways that encourage students to recognize patterns in human behavior and construct meaning through the semiotic relationships between systems and individuals. Students learn most effectively when they are encouraged to not only seek information but also examine its production and mediation within sociocultural contexts. 

I favor an interdisciplinary approach that integrates perspectives of both information science and historical interpretation, particularly scholarship which emphasizes the socially situated nature of knowledge and the distribution of agency within information systems and technological environments. Such research demonstrates that understanding and meaning emerge through active engagement with real-world problems, collaborative inquiry, and critical reflection. As emerging technologies such as generative AI increasingly mediate everyday life, students require not only technical proficiency, but also the analytical and ethical foundations necessary to navigate these systems effectively and responsibly.

My approach to teaching centers on critical inquiry, inclusive classroom practice, and applied learning. I aim to help students build strong analytical skills while connecting course concepts to real-world contexts. I value discussion-based learning, clear scaffolding of assignments, and constructive feedback that supports student growth over time.

I encourage students to engage with complex materials from multiple perspectives, ask thoughtful questions, and develop confidence in their presentation and rhetorical capabilities.
